Not Collaboration

Aside:

Recall that open source is a synonym for free software.

Properly speaking, open-source describes only how software is distributed and used— Not how it is produced.[a] Notably, it requires human-readable "source" be available whenever machine-readable instructions are available. The term does not restrict the organizational structure adopted to produce these works, and many open-source projects are not open to collaboration.

For example, GNU is not collaborative: While users are permitted to modify and redistribute GNU's works, those modifications were never intended to make their way back into GNU source. Hence, there is a deliberate delineation between user and developer.

This development model is now referred to as the Cathedral model, and largely reflects the "firm" or "corporate" structure that had proved most successful at the time.

Collaborative Open Source

When Linus Torvalds invited any interested hacker to tinker on his kernel, he invented a new way of making free software: Changes are submitted by individuals to projects, inspected on a case-by-case basis, and then either accepted into the project or rejected at the project leader's discretion. The idea was "Accidentally Revolutionary," and has dominated open-source since.

This model is by nature distributed, with anyone anywhere welcome to submit (completed) modifications to present works. Project leaders take on the responsibility of their work's overall well-being, and can modify the code however they see fit. Thus, open-source is often collaborative, but it is not a synonym for collaboration.

It should be noted that there is no "rule" that collaboration is restricted to hobbyists, and being inclusive with respect to organizations fosters project success. Facebook, for example, has put considerable effort into Linux's new btrfs,[h] commonly described as a "modern, copy-on-write filesystem."

Moneyed?

Note:

Formally, we will only be considering those qualities shared by both Free Software and Open Source.

All open-source software may be redistributed by any user. This qualification may be explicit, as in the case of copyleft licenses; or, it may be implicit by not forbidding redistribution, as in the case of public domain works. This stipulation does not forbid the sale of open-source software.

While free software is traditionally a hobbyist' production, and is traditionally available free of charge, there are/can-be/will-be moneyed interests seeking to maximize profit by selling open-source products, as in the case of Audacity.

In the first place, the rights of free software apply only to users: While every user has access to source code and may redistribute the work, neither the compiled product nor the source code are necessarily available to the public. Any user, of course, may make both project and source publicly available.

Ownership in Open-Source

Aside:

Here we will restrict our language to the term release.

I prefer instead to identify what is owned in open-source: You the user own your copy of the software, until you decide to release it. Thereupon, any user owns a copy, and may release it themselves— etc etc.. Thus, open source one type of free work.

Probably the most important component is the codebase— the source repository. Again, until this repository is released, its owner decides everything about it, including what goes into it, and when (if) it is released. Linus Torvalds, for example, has the power to never release another kernel; he could simply collect and perfect the source on his own. Point is, the repository maintainer is in no way obliged to respond to any proposed commit— They don't even have to respond to your email.
