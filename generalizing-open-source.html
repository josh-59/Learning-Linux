BONUS: Generalizing Open Source

"If we cannot collectively envision and design a future beyond capitalism,
then there will not be a future that includes us."
—Erin McCarley

As Steven Weber noted in The Success of Open Source, open-source is as much a new and novel way of producing things as it is a collection of software. In this bonus material, I explore one possible method of generalizing its modus operandi to non-software goods.

Gift Economies

Open source is one example of a gift economy— a way of producing things that relies upon giving the results away rather than charging for them.[1][2] Gift economies are characterized by not placing value upon goods and services rendered. Surprisingly, gift economies are fairly common, from an anthropological point of view:

One economy developed over and over by humans on every continent has been the gift economy. In this system, if people have more than they need of anything, they give it away. They don’t assign value, they don’t count debts. Everything you don’t use personally can be given as a gift to someone else, and by giving more gifts you inspire more generosity and strengthen the friendships that keep you swimming in gifts too. Many gift economies lasted for thousands of years, and proved much more effective at enabling all of the participants to meet their needs.[3]

Gift economies are an adaptation to abundance, rather than scarcity.[1] The question is, can we generalize the open source way, and if so, how?

Aside:

This is merely a business whose structure supports volunteerism.

I believe the key lies in the nonprofit whose goal is to give away what it can. The nonprofit seems to me the appropriate device, for a few reasons. First, all open-source projects are nonprofits! It's just that few bother to formally announce it. Second, a nonprofit with an amenable purpose is a natural target for volunteer efforts, both commercial and private. Such non-paying efforts are the backbone of open-source. Third, a collection of nonprofits is realizable within America today— In fact, it is not too far removed from the current way we do things.

Reality Check

Aside:

Transition, not revolution.

For the foreseeable future, any project must be financially solvent to be viable, long-term. That is to say, our goal is to prosper within the status quo— the capitalist / market economy— and not to "revolutionize" anything.

Open source has flourished in the hostile context of the market economy because copying software is very inexpensive. Since copying software costs essentially nothing, the goods produced can be— and are— given away gratis, forming the foundation of (monetarily) free software.

In short, it doesn't have to be cool, it doesn't have to be popular, and it doesn't have to be perfect; but, it does have to be financially solvent.

Many Small- / Medium-Sized Projects

It seems natural that our generalization of open source be composed of many small projects, emulating the success of the "bazaar" model of modern open-source. The first good reason to avoid large projects is that one large, all-inclusive project would be a commune! Communism has been explored, and has consistently proved inferior to the market economy.

Aside:

It should be a goal of every project to grow its own operation while demand remains unsatisfied; there is no contradiction in a nonprofit doing so.

The second reason to prefer small projects is to compartmentalize failure: The failure of one project need not adversely affect the whole. By stripping away that which does not work, what will be left will be a healthy economy.

Third, all healthy economies support competition, and many small projects fosters competition more than a few large ones. That being said, scales of economy should be employed wherever possible.

We will not attempt to describe the internal organization of projects, being as the open-source landscape offers a great deal of diversity in this regard. It is only relevant to us outsiders what the project is producing, and what it is asking of us.

Success in a Gifting Economy

A nonprofit with an amenable goal— and good execution— attracts both volunteer efforts and material donations. Through this mechanism, the organization's operating costs are driven downward, fostering its goal of giving away what it can. Thus, it is the prerogative of the project's leaders to identify goods that other people would be willing to work for, while it is the prerogative of the individual to work for the goods they want.

Aside:

All successful non-profit projects would generate a community.

In particular, each project should seek to attract and support volunteer effort, being as the greatest operating expense of any business is labor.

Volunteer efforts could be part-time, full-time or one-time only; if open source is anything to go by, then we can expect a given project to be manned by a relatively small group of core participants, with the occasional helper stopping by. Note that the nonprofit structure does not exclude paying positions, so that specialists and laborer can be hired, as needed.

With a common, shared goal— the project itself— the contributors to a project would form a community. Such communities would be the bread and butter of a successful gifting economy; without them, the whole edifice fails.

Individual Success in a Gift Economy

The key to success within our alternative economy lies in producing goods and services which are desirable to you. This is our take on "intrinsic motivation". The idea is that if you think they are valuable, then other people will as well, resulting in the creation of value within the whole. Gifting the products away motivates gifting towards the contributors of the product, which is our method of being paid off.

To reiterate, the aim is not to get wealthy by having a high-paying job, but by offering something that justifies reciprocal donations.

Bootstrapping a Gifting Economy

So how do we do it?

Just like a business, every nonprofit requires a plan. The long-term success of a project is its fiscal solvency, so make sure each project stands on its own. There is no contradiction in selling a product to some audience in order to give it away to another.

The first priority should be food, especially, staples:

  • Potatoes
  • Beans
  • Rice
  • Wheat
  • Etc..

To implement this, two projects can be started: One nonprofit to own and manage the farmland, and one (or more) others to farm it. Land not used by any nonprofit can be leased out to local farmers— this is already industry-standard practice.

The resulting products should be given away as each nonprofit can sustain it:

  • To those who show up and help out first
  • To those who donate second
  • To those in the vicinity third

Farming is labor-intensive for perhaps three months out of the year, and our second priority should be housing. We can expect workers to be motivated for such buildings, so that they can be built by volunteer efforts. Again, the nonprofits responsible for housing should take care to remain fiscally solvent, and gift with discretion.

The third priority must be a means of getting around. There's no reason a nonprofit could not own buses to move people to and from their worksites. At the personal level, though, I do not expect privately owned vehicles (meaning cars and trucks) to be commonplace— Rather, I expect the bicycle to be the main way of getting around.

Aside:

To make use of volunteer efforts, materials must always be available. At the lowest level, this means land must be owned by nonprofit organizations for exploitation.

If you had these three things, you would be giving people the oportunity to volunteer. If you then made tools and materials available, people could build what they want. Although it may seem trivial, we can rely upon the wants and needs of the public to dictate what projects are worth pursuing, for the motivation to work will only come from the people themselves.

Aside:

It is the responsibility of the nonprofit to organize said tools and materials.

I fully expect there to be hardships as this alternative economy gets on its feet— Things like substandard housing, boring diets, a lack of videogames to play, lack of heat, etc. are to be expected. But these things can be overcome, and I fully expect the result to prove superior— more sustainable, less alienating, less dependant upon constant growth— than the status quo.

Communication is key, and there should exist some means of organizing labor— Ideally, an app. Every nonprofit should be very vocal about what it's doing, what it needs, and in inviting people to participate in its mission. Make the work a fun, social affair; I would love to see a 6-hour/day, 4-day/week workweek become standard!

Why?

Because the open-source way has produced better products the proprietary method of development.

Maybe it's crazy. Maybe it's stupid— People don't buy things they don't want, after all. But maybe there are others that feel the same way I do— that our collective pursuit of profit has resulted in unsustainable, unsatisfying lives.

As David Graeber wrote,

"There is a way out, which is to accept that anarchist forms of organization would not look anything like a state. That they would involve an endless variety of communities, associations, networks, projects, on every conceivable scale, overlapping and intersecting in any way we could imagine, and possibly many that we can’t. Some would be quite local, others global. Perhaps all they would have in common is that none would involve anyone showing up with weapons and telling everyone else to shut up and do what they were told. And that, since anarchists are not actually trying to seize power within any national territory, the process of one system replacing the other will not take the form of some sudden revolutionary cataclysm— the storming of a Bastille, the seizing of a Winter Palace— but will necessarily be gradual, the creation of alternative forms of organization on a world scale, new forms of communication, new, less alienated ways of organizing life, which will, eventually, make currently existing forms of power seem stupid and beside the point. That in turn would mean that there are endless examples of viable anarchism: pretty much any form of organization would count as one, so long as it was not imposed by some higher authority, from a klezmer band to the international postal service."

In the future I see, your life will not be defined by the kind of work you do. You'll work, sure, when the need arises or the inclination strikes you. But not eight hours a day, and not forty hours a week.

Maybe you'll assemble bicycles at the local bike shop. Maybe you'll harvest apples from the local trees. Maybe you'll attend some lectures at the local university. Maybe you'll mow the grass or just play with your kids.

Your life will be yours to live as you see fit, not bound by the pursuit of money, and not bound to please corporate overlords. I dream of an all-volunteer workforce, obtained through non-revolutionary means, where everybody wins, and nobody works a bullshit job. You don't have to file useless paperwork in some office building somewhere— you can build things that matter to you! And you can do so in a way that contributes to the happiness of your fellow man!

Since our goal is to give stuff away, and not to turn a profit on it, what will result is a profound re-thinking of the society we all take for granted. Consider, for example, laundry soap. It would naturally be distributed in bulk (i.e., palletized containers), and left to the consumer to fill their own container from it. This eliminates the single-use plastic associated with laundry detergent today.

In other words, the seemingly innocuous change of working for products per se naturally leads to less work, greater efficiency and increased sustainability. In this way, I am interested in creating a society that can be trusted. For, I doubt that engineers, left to their own devices, would create something as asinine as a pickup truck that destroys the planet. No, it's only within the context of profit-driven corporations and conspicuous spending that such barbaric entities exist.

This is a society by us, for us; our goal is to demonstrate that such a society is superior to the current way of doing things. The way to do so is to gradually shift from the old way to the new way. Ideally, we would blur the line between "hobby" and "work", for the benefit of all— Notably, you don't have to volunteer 40 hours a week!

FAQ

Aside:

Anyway, I'm an anarchist, not a communist.

Question: Is this communism?
Answer: No. Under this method, the "means of production" are not owned by the state, nor are they owned communally. Rather, they are owned by organizations (specifically, nonprofits), which bear the responsibility of tending them. This is very similar (indeed, only slightly different from) the current organization of things (in which "means of production" are owned by corporations).

Question: Then what's different?
Answer: Rather than seeking profits for shareholders, organizations would seek to give away as much as possible. They do so by systematically producing and gifting goods to the people and organizations that need them.

Question: Do you support a workers' revolution?
Answer: No. I don't believe in revolution, and the reason is strictly pragmatic: Revolution is a good way to make a big fat mess of things. Rather, I believe in the slow and careful rethinking of things, with a focus on sustainability and an eye towards long-term wealth generation.

Question: Won't this stir a revolution?
Answer: No. Imagine for a moment that the cost of Ramen Noodles dropped to zero, overnight. What effect would that have? Would people start hoarding noodles? Sure, but only to an extent. Ramen noodles, while perfectly capable of keeping an individual from starving, are not especially desirable to eat, especially long-term. No, the effect would be minor at best. This is the sort of change that we seek to catalyze.

Question: What about cooperatives?
Answer: A cooperative is an organization owned by its workers, and so represents a restructuring of the ownership of the organization. Since the workers (whom are also the owners) seek their own best interests, it is not fundamentally different from corporate management, and thus not of interest to us.

Question: Isn't this a bit of a dream?
Answer: Well, yes... But it's a dream that has been broken down into manageable parts, and ought therefore be realizable.

Question: When?
Answer: When members of the public are motivated to do it. This motivation must be felt, rather than speculated about.
