BONUS: Generalizing Open Source

As Steven Weber noted in The Success of Open Source, open-source is less a collection of software than a new and novel way of producing things. In this bonus material, I explore one possible method of generalizing it to non-software goods and services.

Reality Check

Aside:

Transition, not revolution.

For the foreseeable future, any project must be financially solvent to be viable, long-term. That is, our goal is to prosper within the status quo— the capitalist, market economy— and not to "revolutionize" the workplace.

Open source has flourished because copying software is very inexpensive. Since copying software costs next to nothing, the goods produced can be— and are— given away gratis. The question is, can we generalize its modus operondi, and if so, how?

The key lies in the nonprofit whose goal is to give away as much as possible. The nonprofit seems to me the appropriate device, for a few reasons:

  • Supports Volunteerism
    • Is a natural target for volunteer efforts
  • Supports Gifting
    • Both to the project and from the project
  • Intrinsic Value

Doesn't have to be cool, doesn't have to be popular, but it does have to be financially solvent. For example, there could be a nonprofit whose aim is to give away as much housing as it can. Apartment buildings could be owned by this nonprofit, and this project would be led by someone.

Many Small Projects

It seems natural that our alternative to the market economy should be composed of many small projects, rather than few large ones. The first good reason to avoid large projects is that one large, all-inclusive project would be a commune! Communism has been explored and has failed.

The second reason to prefer many small projects is to compartmentalize failure: The failure of one project need not adversely affect the whole.

Third, a healthy economy supports competition, and many small projects would foster competition more so than a few large projects.

Mutual Gifting

The key to this theory is mutual gifting among projects. Rather than seeking profits, proprietors of nonprofits would seek wealth through the gifting from other projects. To do that, they must produce goods valuable enough to warrant the gifting.

This is our take on "intrinsic value."

Through this mechanism, the nonprofit's operating costs are driven downward, fostering its goal of giving away as much as possible.

Punishing Misbehavior

Naturally, the creator of each article is entitled to gift their goods to whomever they want— and to decline gifting for whatever reason whatsoever, including loss of trust.

Viewed in the abstract, each project makes a change: Be it from land into iron ore (as in a mine); or iron ore into steel (as in a foundry); or steel into consumer products (as in a factory).

Why?

I don't like the status quo! It's full of idiots seeking status among other idiots, principally by way of conspicuous spending.

On a deeper level, I am interested in creating a society that can be trusted. For, I doubt that engineers, left to their own devices, would create something as asinine as a pickup truck that destroys the planet. No, it's only within the context of greed and profit-driven corporations that such a barbaric entity makes sense.

I dream of an all-volunteer workforce, obtained through nonrevolutionary means, where everybody wins, and nobody works a bullshit job. I dream of sprawling, liveable, medium-density cities, built upon sound, mixed-use zoning practices.

Naturally, the tenents of open-source would be taken advantage of wherever possible— e.g., open-source designs along with transparent and well-documented production methods.

Potentially, more material wealth for the average citizen Not that far-fetched. I see a collection of open-source designs and production methods, and people volunteering to produce these things, for their own sake.
