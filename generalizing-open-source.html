BONUS: Generalizing Open Source

As Steven Weber noted in The Success of Open Source, open-source is less a collection of software than a new, novel way of producing things. In this bonus material, I explore one possible method of generalizing it to non-software goods and services.

Reality Check

Aside:

Transition, not revolution.

For the foreseeable future, any project must be financially solvent to be viable, long-term. That is, our goal is to prosper within the status quo— the capitalist, market economy— and not to "revolutionize" the workplace.

Aside:

It doesn't have to be cool; it doesn't have to be popular; it doesn't have to be perfect; but, it does have to be financially solvent.

Open source has flourished in this context because copying software is very inexpensive. Since copying software costs next to nothing, the goods produced can be— and are— given away gratis. The question is, can we generalize its modus operandi, and if so, how?

I believe the key lies in the nonprofit whose goal is to give away as much as possible. Such nonprofits seem to me the appropriate device, for a few reasons.

First, all open-source projects are nonprofits! It's just that the cost of copying software is so low that few bother to formally announcing it.

Second, a nonprofit with an ameniable goal is a natural target for volunteer efforts, both commercial and private. For example, an automobile manufacturer might find it in their best interest to donate time and money to the development of new tooling, and thereby support a project other than themselves.

Third, a nonprofit is a natural target for volunteer effort, both the real and ideal backbone of open-source.

Many Small Projects

It seems natural that our generalization of open source should be composed of many small projects, rather than a few large ones. The first good reason to avoid large projects is that one large, all-inclusive project would be a commune! Communism has been explored, and has failed.

The second reason to prefer small projects is to compartmentalize failure: The failure of one project need not adversely affect the whole. By stripping away that which does not work, what will be left will be a healthy economy.

Third, a healthy economy supports competition, and many small projects would foster competition more so than a few large projects.

We will not attempt to describe the internal organization of projects, being as the open-source landscape offers a great deal of diversity in this regard.

Mutual Gifting

The key to long-term wealth generation lies in the mutual gifting by projects, and among projects. By offering desirable goods or services, a nonprofit would attract both volunteer efforts and materiel donations. Through this mechanism, the nonprofit's operating costs are driven downward, fostering its goal of giving away as much as possible.

One economy developed over and over by humans on every continent has been the gift economy. In this system, if people have more than they need of anything, they give it away. They don’t assign value, they don’t count debts. Everything you don’t use personally can be given as a gift to someone else, and by giving more gifts you inspire more generosity and strengthen the friendships that keep you swimming in gifts too. Many gift economies lasted for thousands of years, and proved much more effective at enabling all of the participants to meet their needs. Capitalism may have drastically increased productivity, but to what end? On one side of your typical capitalist city someone is starving to death while on the other side someone is eating caviar.

Thus, it is the prerogative of each project leader to determine what good or service to offer, to maximize gifting to the project; this is our take on "intrinsic value". In particular, it should be the goal of each project to attract and support volunteerism, being as the greatest operating expense of any business is labor.

To reiterate, the goal is not to get rich by having a high-paying job, but to get wealthy by attracting donations in the form of gifts.

Punishing Misbehavior

Naturally, the creator of each article is entitled to gift their goods to whomever they want— and to decline gifting for any reason whatsoever, including loss of trust. This forms the foundation of disciplinary action: If we don't like you, we won't give to you.

One good reason not to support a project is lack of transparency, especially, fiscal transparency. Thereby Accountability Let someone else handle the books.

Such excommunication has the potential to form a motivating force ("Well I had better get off my ass and do something").

American Alternative

To simplify matters for the general public, there should exist a class of oversight organization, whose job it is to inspect and verify the validity of other organizations.

"We are American Alternative" - Nonprofit organization - Goal is to give away as much as possible - Fiscally transparent - Is a valid target of gifting, both in terms of labor and materials

"We support American Alternative" - Gives to an American Alternative organization (as above). - Is a valid target of gifting, both in terms of labor and materials.

Why?

I don't like the status quo! It's full of idiots seeking status among other idiots, principally by way of conspicuous spending.

On a deeper level, I am interested in creating a society that can be trusted. For, I doubt that engineers, left to their own devices, would create something as asinine as a pickup truck that destroys the planet. No, it's only within the context of profit-driven corporations and conspicuous spending that such a barbaric entity makes sense.

I dream of an all-volunteer workforce, obtained through nonrevolutionary means, where everybody wins, and nobody works a bullshit job. You don't have to make some useless widget, some fidget spinner, you can do things that are important!

Like Richard Stallman, my principal aim is to prove that a new, and different, culture can succeed in a hostile environment. My hope is that by building on what we know, we can usher in a sound, science-based, sustainable, and agreeable way of life.

Naturally, the tenents of open-source would be taken advantage of wherever possible— e.g., open-source designs along with transparent and well-documented production methods.
