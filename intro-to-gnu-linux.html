Introduction to GNU/Linux

"Those who do not understand UNIX
are condemned to reinvent it, poorly."
Henry Spence, Programmer

An operating system is a collection of software which facilitates the use of a computer. To this end, its two main responsibilities are to support running programs, called processes, and to maintain persistent data, in the form of files. In addition, operating systems ease the task of creating useful programs by offering programmers a logical architecture and predictable environment. Free and open source GNU/Linux has evolved to offer the single best programming environment available today.

UNIX: A Brief History

The UNIX operating system was born in late 1969 at AT&T's Bell Laboratories, in the shadow of Multics. Multics was an operating system intended to commercialize computing by offering it as a service, similar to telephone or electricity.[1] The intent was for users to connect terminals to a central mainframe, then pay for computation on an as-used basis. For a variety of reasons, the project never realized its dream, and in 1969 AT&T abandoned it.

Within Bell Labs, management put operating systems research on the backburner, and refused to purchase new computing equipment.[2][3] After finding a disused PDP-7, Kenneth Thompson and Dennis Ritchie rewrote Space Travel for it; after, Thompson continued to work with the PDP-7, implementing a filesystem he and others at Bell Laboratories had been devising on paper.[3]

At some point in 1969, while working on this filesystem, Thompson realized that he was very close to an operating system: All he needed was an assembler, a text editor, a shell and an exec system call.[2] The exec call was "trivial", and the other three components were done in one week each.[2]

About his colleague's work, Dennis Ritchie said,

Thompson wanted to create a comfortable computing environment constructed according to his own design, using whatever means were available. His plans, it is evident in retrospect, incorporated many of the innovative aspects of Multics, including an explicit notion of a process as a locus of control, a tree-structured file system, a command interpreter as user-level program, simple representation of text files, and generalized access to devices.[4]

The new operating system soon became a communal affair, and in 1970 was christened UNIX, as a "somewhat treacherous pun on MULTICS."[2][3] The earliest versions were put to use internally by Bell Labs to assist in word processing and patent applications.[2][3] The software then moved away from its nest under obtuse licenses to universities, where its source code, though not free, was studied and documented.[2] Finally, UNIX became commercially available, to principally corporate clients.[2]

The UNIX Architecture

UNIX had a clear, simple, and well-documented architecture that inspired many operating systems, including GNU/Linux. It is organized as a heirarchy of layers; each layer builds upon the one beneathe it, and supports the layer immediately above it.

Hardware: A computer's hardware can be likened to its body. Each piece of hardware has a software interface defined by the engineers that created it; these interfaces are varied at best.

Drivers: Drivers interact with hardware, and simplify the interface between kernel and hardware. This software represents the deepest and most remote element of any operating system, and the abstraction layer it offers allows a single kernel to run on a variety of machines.

Kernel: The heart of any UNIX-like operating system is its kernel. The kernel is responsible for supporting running programs, scheduling the CPU, allotting memory, and maintaining file integrity, in addition to many other tasks. It is a large and involved piece of software, and can be likened to the "brains" of the operation.

System Calls: The set of operations that the kernel can undertake is defined by the system calls it supports. UNIX-like operating systems have six essential system calls: fork, exec, open, close, read, and write.[5] Both fork and exec are concerned with launching programs, while open, close, read, and write constitute the essential interactions a process can have with files. Processes cannot dictate how the kernel carries these operations out, and in fact, the kernel may deny one if, for instance, a file does not exist, or the calling process does not have appropriate authority.

Processes: A process, sometimes called a "user-space process," is an executing program. Within UNIX and like operating systems, processes run the show: The kernel is entirely dependant upon processes to tell it what to do, and so has no "mind" of its own.

Free as in Freedom

Richard Stallman

The first attempt at producing a deliberately free and open-source UNIX clone was announced by Richard Stallman in 1983. Stallman had been working at MIT's AI Laboratory for over a decade, and had grown accustomed to its culture of sharing source code:

"If you saw someone using an unfamiliar and interesting program, you could always ask to see the source code, so that you could read it, change it, or cannibalize parts of it to make a new program."[6]

In 1981, that culture collapsed as commercial ventures hired away most of the lab.[6][7] In 1982, when administrators purchased a new PDP-10, they opted for a closed-source, proprietary operating system, and indirectly threatened to seal the fate of software freedom. Stallman began to consider the merits of sharing software in itself, in the process deriving an analogy to scientific cooperation, a pillar of the University's morals.[8][9]

Yet, proprietary software was becoming the norm, with students working on projects intended for profit[8] and companies going so far as to require nondisclosure agreements be signed to get an executable.[6] On September 27, 1983, Stallman announced his resolution: To produce a "complete UNIX-compatible software system," and to "give it away free."[10] He called the project GNU, a recursive acronym for GNU's Not UNIX.[11]

Aside:

These ideas are expressed formally and generally through copyleft.

At the time of announcement, Stallman had not yet quantified his own intentions behind the word free. Certainly, "a free copy" did not embody the culture of code-sharing MIT was once home to. More, he did not wsih to put the code in the public domain because modified versions of it it could become proprietary.[12] By 1985, Stallman had sorted these issues out:

GNU is not in the public domain. Everyone will be permitted to modify and redistribute GNU, but no distributor will be allowed to restrict its further redistribution. That is to say, proprietary modifications will not be allowed. I want to make sure that all versions of GNU remain free.[13]

Thus, software freedom became quantified: Users can run, study, modify, copy and distribute the software; but, if they distribute, then those freedoms must be preserved. These freedoms were implemented in the various licenses of early GNU products, and their parts were taken together to form the GNU General Public License, version 1.[14]

About it, Linus Torvalds writes:

"Under the terms of the GPL, money is not the issue. You can charge a million bucks if someone is willing to pay for it, but you have to make sources available. And the person you give or sell the source to has to have all the rights you have. It's a brilliant device."[5]

Aside:

GNU's own kernel, Hurd, has not, as of 2021, been completed.

To create an entire operating system is an enormous task, but by 1990, the GNU Project had either found or written all major components except one— the kernel.[11] Today, the GNU Project is renowned for producing high-quality, well-documented free software; they are as "official" as the open-source community gets. They remain active and prominent among the free software community, fighting the good fight with the help of the Free Software Foundation.[15] Richard Stallman remains Chief GNUisance.[15]

The Linux Kernel

On August 25, 1991, a Finnish graduate student by the name of Linus Torvalds announced his own free operating system project. Again following the architecture of the UNIX operating system, his project was intended to be "...just a hobby, won't be big and professional like GNU."[16]

Aside:

Properly speaking, the name Linux refers only to the Linux kernel.

Whereas the GNU Project sought to produce an entire operating system, Linus restricted his attention to creating a kernel. By October of 1991, GNU's Bash, GCC, Make and other programs were running on his creation.[16] He soon released it under the GNU General Public License, Version 2. Later, he would write:

"The fact is, to make Linux usable, I had relied on a lot of tools that had been distributed freely over the Internet—I had hoisted myself up on the shoulders of giants. The most important of these free software programs was the GCC compiler."[5]

Aside:

Linus Torvalds became interested in operating systems after finding an error in the source code of his Sinclair QL's QDOS. [5] In college, Andrew Tanenbaum's Operating Systems: Design and Implementation took this interest to new heights.[5]

Interest in the kernel flourished, in large part because of the informality of the project: Everybody was invited, if only to tinker. This development model— "release early and often, delegate everything you can, be open to the point of promiscuity"[17]— raised eyebrows at the time, but has proven sound in virtue of its overwhelming success: The Linux kernel has become the most-used UNIX-like kernel of all time.

The kernel development process has become more structured since 1991, and today uses a train-schedule approach. Linus Torvalds remains the maintainer of the Development branch, and often remarks upon the kernel's latest releases.

A major figure of today's Linux development effort is Greg Kroah-Hartman (pronounced "Crow-Hartman"), who maintains the Stable branch, in addition to various subsystems.[18] According to Kroah-Hartman, interest in the kernel continues to grow, with 1200 new developers contributing each year.[19] Their main development bottlenecks are code review and maintainership.[19]

Although the Linux kernel began as a hobby, it is an occupation for some, and serious affair for many. In particular, the kernel development team takes great care not to break user-space,[20] meaning that programs that did work will continue to work. In this way, they ensure the long-term stability of Linux-based systems.[21]

Linux Distributions: A Brief History

The first versions of the Linux kernel were available only in source-code form, and required compilation on a host machine.[16] More, no other software was included, so that the creation of a functional Linux-based machine required the user to obtain, configure, compile and assemble a number of disparate software components.

HJ Lu's Boot/Root Floppies

In 1992, HJ Lu began packaging utilities alongside the kernel and distributing the collection together on a pair of 5.25" disks.[22][23] Known as boot/root floppies, they are the first known Linux distribution,[23] and worked similarly to a live CD: You booted from one disk, then replaced it when prompted. "After a while," says Joe Klemmer, "you got a command prompt."[24]

Linux's first widely recognized and used distribution was, ironically, a commercial venture: Soft Landing System.[24] Released in late 1992, SLS was sold under the slogan "Gentle Touchdowns for DOS Bailouts",[25] but users complained that the software was buggy,[25][26] and when its developers decided to change the executable file format, began to leave.[14]

Two Approaches

In late 1992, Patrick Volkerding needed a LISP interpreter for a project at school, and, having heard that clisp ran on SLS Linux, downloaded them both.[26] His professor got wind, and asked Patrick to help him install the software on school computers.[26] Volkerding walked him through installing, fixing and patching SLS, and afterward, his professor asked, "Is there some way we can fix the install disks so that the new machines will have these fixes right away?"[26]

So Volkerding began improving SLS, and internally named his project Slackware; he didn't want the project taken too seriously.[26] By May, though, the improvements were becoming substantial, and when first released on the internet in June, proved so popular that the number of download requests crashed the system hosting it.[26] Volkerding hadn't intended to produce a distribution,[26] but still maintains Slackware today.[27] The Slackware Linux Project is aimed at producing, "the most UNIX-like Linux distribution out there."[28]

Slackware 1.01, released 1993

Aside:

Debian is one of the most respected distributions of our time, and is oft-used as an upstream source for other distributions.

In 1993, Ian Murdock also grew frustrated with SLS Linux,[29] and, more, sought a better solution to distribution creation than single-person or closed-room schemes.[29] Ian wanted an operating system that lived up to the Linux name, was carefully and conscientiously assembled, and which was maintained with equal diligence.[29] In particular, he wanted a free operating system that could compete with commercial distributions.[29]

Murdock decided to make the process open, in the spirit of GNU and Linux, thereby ensuring satisfaction of user's requirements.[29] He named it Debian, a portmanteau of Debra, his wife, and Ian, himself.[30] The project attracted developers, and today around a thousand people volunteer their spare time.[31]

Debian 4 "Etch", released 2007

With the internet came many Linux distributions. I think of them as flavors of the same thing, rather than necessarily different. Since they are each based around the Linux kernel, and the kernel has the property that it does not change behavior across versions, they are fundamentally similar operating systems.

Linux Mint

Aside:

Mint is one of several Debian derivatives.

I like Linux Mint, but then, I'm a simple guy. Mint is a characteristically professional operating system, and focuses on productivity— that means you'll be up and running before you know it. It is extremely new-user friendly, yet maintains the performance of any other UNIX-like operating system.

For example, Mint 19 introduced Timeshift:

"Timeshift protects your system by taking incremental snapshots of the file system at regular intervals. These snapshots can be restored at a later date to undo all changes to the system."

Mint offers the Cinnamon desktop environment; Windows users will feel right at home. I find it to be an attractive desktop, but it's only moderately customizable. Like many users, I want a desktop environment that stays out of my way, and Cinnamon does exactly that.

Linux Mint 20.1 "Ulyssa", Released 2021

When an example operating system is required in this book, Linux Mint will be used, but similar remarks apply to virtually any distribution, with a little perusing.

Closing Remarks:

Open Source

In the forty years since Richard Stallman began GNU (pronounced guh-nu), the free software landscape has seen a great deal of growth. In 1998, the term open source was invented as a less abrasive synonym for free software:

We realized it was time to dump the confrontational attitude that has been associated with 'free software' in the past and sell the idea strictly on the same pragmatic, business-case grounds that motivated Netscape.[32]

The result was a wealth of media attention, the Open Source Initiative and Mozilla. Both GNU and the OSI agree that their respective terms are nearly synonymous.[33][34] In this work, and in life, we will use "open-source" interchangeably with "free software".

Aside:

Today, the most popular open-source license is MIT's, by far.[35]

In addition, more developers have taken part, and introduced a number of licenses to suit their needs. These licenses can be divided into two classes: Permissive licenses (e.g., MIT, Apache, BSD) do not perpetuate themselves, and so indirectly allow derivate works to become proprietary; nonpermissive/copyleft licenses (e.g., GPL, MPL), on the other hand, do perpetuate themselves.[36][37]

To be honest, a lot of developers have said, To hell with this, and introduced such novelties as the Beerware License, the Do What The Fuck You Want License, and the Unlicense (quite popular). These each, with varying degrees of professionalism, place code in the public domain; from there, anyone can claim it as their own, and can put it in any other work, under any other license.
