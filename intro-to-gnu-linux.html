An operating system is a collection of software designed to facilitate the use of a computer. To this end, its two main responsibilities are to support running programs, known as processes, and to maintain persistent data, usually in the form of files. In addition, operating systems ease the task of creating useful programs (i.e., computer programming). Modern GNU/Linux offers the single best programming environment available today.

GNU/Linux Architecture

The GNU/Linux operating system has a clear and well-documented architecture, composed of several distinct layers:

At the heart of any machine is its hardware. Each piece of hardware has a software interface defined by the engineers that created it. These interfaces are varied at best.

Directly interacting with hardware and providing a more-uniform interface to each piece is the task of the system's drivers. This software layer represents the deepest and most remote element of any operating system. While drivers can be installed on an as-needed basis, in Linux the tendency is to include many drivers, and use only those that are required.

While the heart of any machine is its hardware, the heart of any GNU/Linux operating system is its kernel. The kernel uses drivers to direct the actions of the machine, at the behest of user-space programs. Because the expectations that we have for the system are great (running multiple programs concurrently, never losing files nor parts of files, weeks-long stability), this is a large and involved piece of software.

The Linux kernel was began by Linus Torvalds, then a graduate student of the University of Helsinki.[1] Following the architecture of the Unix operating system, he wrote an open-source kernel as "...a hobby, won't be big and professional like [the GNU kernel]." He soon released it publicly under the free-and-open-source GNU General Public License, version 2.
