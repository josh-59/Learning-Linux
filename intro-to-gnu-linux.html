Introduction to GNU/Linux

"Those who do not understand UNIX
are condemned to reinvent it, poorly."
Henry Spence, Programmer

An operating system is a collection of software which facilitates the use of a computer. To this end, its two main responsibilities are to support running programs, callled processes, and to maintain persistent data, in the form of files. In addition, operating systems ease the task of creating useful programs by offering programmers a logical architecture and predictable environment. GNU/Linux has evolved to offer the single best programming environment available today.

UNIX

The Unix Operating System was born in late 1969 at Bell Labs, in the shadow of Multics. Multics was an operating system intended to commercialize computing by offering it as a service, similar to telephone or electricity.[1] The intent was for users to connect terminals to a central mainframe, then pay for computation on an as-used basis.

Being a joint venture between MIT, AT&T's Bell Laboratories, and General Electric, Multics was a large and, in many ways, novel, project. It introduced many new operating systems ideas— among them, the hierarchical file structure.[1] For a variety of reasons, the project never realized its dream, and in 1969 Bell Laboratories abandoned it.

Within Bell Labs, management put operating systems research on the backburner, and refused to purchase new computing equipment.[2][3] After finding a disused PDP-7, Ken Thompson rewrote Space Travel for it.[2][3] In the process, he accrued the tools and skills necessary to implement a filesystem he and others at Bell Laboratories had been devising on paper.[3]

At some point in 1969, while working on his filesystem, Ken Thompson realized that he was three weeks from an operating system. All he needed was an exec call, an assembler, a text editor and a shell. In 1970, the new operating system was christened UNIX, as a "somewhat treacherous pun on MULTICS."[3]

Owing in part to the restrictive nature of available hardware, the budding operating system focused on simplicity.[4] The earliest versions of the new operating system were put to use internally by Bell Labs to assist in patent applications.[2][3] The software then moved away from its nest under obtuse licenses to universities, where its source code, though not free, was studied and documented.[2] Finally, Unix became commercially available, to principally to corporate clients.[2]

The Unix Architecture

Unix had a simple, clear and well-documented architecture that inspired many operating systems, including GNU/Linux. It is organized as a heirarchy of layers: Each layer builds upon the one beneathe it, and supports the layer immediately above it.

Hardware: At the heart of any computer is its hardware. Each piece of hardware has a software interface defined by the engineering team that created it. These interfaces are varied at best.

Drivers: Drivers interact with hardware, and provide the kernel with a more-uniform interface to each piece. This software layer represents the deepest and most remote element of any operating system. Although drivers can be installed on an as-needed basis, in GNU/Linux the tendency is to include many drivers, and use only those that are required. Drivers are not usually written by the hardware's engineers or vendors.

Kernel: While the heart of any computer is its hardware, the heart any Unix-like operating system is its kernel. The kernel is responsible for scheduling the CPU, allotting memory, supporting running programs and maintaining file integrity; it is a large and involved piece of software.

System Calls: The operations that the kernel can undertake are, classically, defined by the system calls it supports. Unix-like operating systems have six essential system calls: fork, exec, open, close, read, and write.[5] Both fork and exec are concerned with processes, while open, close, read, and write are concerned with files. These effectively permit interaction with the kernel, and it is often useful to know how the kernel responds to system calls. However, processes cannot dictate how the kernel carries system calls out; in fact, the kernel may deny a system call (if, say, a file does not exist, or the calling process does not have appropriate authority).

Processes: A process, sometimes called a "user-space process" or "user-land process," is an executing program. Within Unix and other Unix-like operating systems, processes run the entire show: The kernel is entirely dependant upon processes to tell it what to do. It follows that there must exist at least one "trusted" process within user-land, to get other processes on their feet— and this is exactly the case. This is one of the more beautiful elements of the Unix architecture.

Free as in Freedom

The first attempt at producing a deliberately free and open-source Unix clone was announced by Richard Stallman in 1983. Stallman was, at the time, a graduate student at MIT's AI Laboratory,[6] and his project was called "GNU," a recursive acronym for GNU's Not Unix.[7]

To create an entire operating system is an enormous task, but, seven years later, the GNU Project had either found or written all major components– except one, the kernel.[7] GNU's own kernel, dubbed Hurd, has yet to be completed.

From gnu.org:

“Free software” means software that respects users' freedom and community. Roughly, it means that the users have the freedom to run, copy, distribute, study, change and improve the software.[sic]

Today, the GNU Project is well-regarded for producing high-quality, well-documented free software; they are as "official" as the open-source community gets. In particular, their compiler collection, gcc, contains the de-facto C compiler of the open-source community, if not the world. It is the only compiler capable of building Linux kernels.[8]

GNU General Public License

Of arguably greater importance than the software created by the GNU Project was the license invented to release it under. Called the GNU General Public License, software released under it ensures its users' freedoms.

About the device, Linus Torvalds writes:

"Under the terms of the GPL, money is not the issue. You can charge a million bucks if someone is willing to pay for it, but you have to make sources available. And the person you give or sell the source to has to have all the rights you have. It's a brilliant device."[5]

GNU remains both active and prominent among the free software community, fighting the good fight with the help of the Free Software Foundation.[9] Richard Stallman remains Chief GNUisance.[9]

The Linux Kernel

On August 25, 1991, a Finnish graduate student by the name of Linus Torvalds announced his own free operating system project. Again following the architecture of the Unix operating system, his project was intended to be "...just a hobby, won't be big and professional like GNU."[10]

Aside:

Properly speaking, the name Linux refers only to the Linux kernel.

While the GNU Project sought to produce an entire operating system, Linus restricted his attention to creating a kernel. By October of 1991, GNU's Bash, GCC, Make and other programs were running on his creation.[10] He soon released it under the GNU General Public License, Version 2. Later, he would write:

"The fact is, to make Linux usable, I had relied on a lot of tools that had been distributed freely over the Internet—I had hoisted myself up on the shoulders of giants. The most important of these free software programs was the GCC compiler."[5]

Aside:

Linus Torvalds became interested in operating systems after finding an error in the source code of his Sinclair QL's QDOS. [5] In college, Andrew Tanenbaum's Operating Systems: Design and Implementation took this interest to new heights.[5]

Interest in the kernel flourished, in large part because of the informality of the project: Everybody was invited, if only to tinker. This development model— "release early and often, delegate everything you can, be open to the point of promiscuity"[11]— raised eyebrows at the time, but has proven sound in virtue of its overwhelming success: The Linux kernel has become the most successful Unix-like kernel of all time, and accounts for fully half of the "GNU/Linux" namesake.

The kernel development process has become (somewhat) more structured since 1991, and today uses an (organic) train-schedule approach. Linus Torvalds remains the maintainer of the Development branch, and often remarks upon the kernel's latest releases.

A major figure of today's Linux development team is Greg Kroah-Hartman (pronounced "Crow-Hartman"), who maintains the Stable branch, in addition to various subsystems.[12] According to Mr. Kroah-Hartman, interest in the kernel continues to grow, with 1200 new developers contributing each year.[13] Their main development bottlenecks are code review and maintainership.[13]

Although the Linux kernel began as a hobby, it is an occupation for some, and serious affair for many. In particular, the kernel development team takes great care not to break user-space,[14] meaning that programs that did work will continue to work. In this way, they ensure the long-term stability of Linux-based systems.[15]

Linux Distributions

A Linux distribution is a collection of software that, when installed, yields a functioning operating system. Each includes some variant of the Linux kernel, some core utilities, and, usually, some other user-space applications (e.g., Firefox). The collection is tested to work and packaged in a fashion that facilitates installation (usually, an ISO disk image); because the assemblers of these operating systems do not typically lay claim to the software, it is more accurate to say that they are distributing others' software.

Linux distributions are more similar than dissimilar. They tend not to be strictly incompatible operating systems, and so Think of them as flavors of the Unix architecture, rather than strictly incompatible operating systems. The oldest distribution that's still maintained is Slackware, first released in 1993.[16]

Nerd Heaven:

Nearly all GNU/Linux distributions ship with programming tools installed— Expect to find gcc, python, make and others ready to go!

The creators and maintainers of a distribution often have precise goals for their project. For instance, the Debian project intends to produce a stable operating system appropriate for use in servers, while the Fedora project wishes to stay closer to the cutting edge, and is willing to sacrifice some stability to do so.

Almost always, a distribution becomes a project, around which a community forms. These communities usually produce tangentially related software (such as installation mechanisms), and provide support and documentation for their work.

Fun Fact:

SteamOS is downstream of Debian 8.

When browsing for your next new operating system, you will often hear the term "downstream," which warrants some explanation. Since these projects are free software, it is permitted to copy, then modify it; in the case of Linux distributions, such a modification is called an derivative, and the modified software is said to be downstream of its source.

For instance, Ubuntu is downstream of Debian, and Manjaro is downstream of Arch. Usually, these derivatives focus on quality-of-life improvements, and as a rule of thumb, you can trust free software.

Linux Mint

Aside:

Mint is downstream of Ubuntu, so all remarks and examples in this text apply to it as well.

I like Linux Mint, but then, I'm a simple guy. Mint is a characteristically professional operating system, and focuses on productivity— that means you'll be up and running before you know it. It is extremely new-user friendly, and maintains the power of any other Unix-like operating system.

New for Linux Mint 19 is Timeshift. From their github: "Timeshift protects your system by taking incremental snapshots of the file system at regular intervals. These snapshots can be restored at a later date to undo all changes to the system."

Aside:

It has been said that one can spend a lifetime customizing Linux desktops.

Mint offers the Cinnamon desktop environment; Windows users will feel right at home. I find it to be an attractive desktop, but it's only moderately customizable. Like many users, I just want a desktop environment that stays out of my way, and Cinnamon does exactly that.

Mint isn't perfect: It tends to be boring. Changes from release to release are minor, and the software available from Debian's repositories tends to be mature and stable, as opposed to new and unproven.
