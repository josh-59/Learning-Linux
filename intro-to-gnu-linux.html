Introduction to GNU/Linux

"Those who do not understand UNIX
are condemned to reinvent it, poorly."
Henry Spence, Programmer

An operating system is a piece of software which facilitates the use of a computer. To this end, its two main responsibilities are to support running programs and to maintain persistent data. GNU/Linux belongs to a large class of operating systems known as UNIX-like, having an architecture, design philosophy, and system-call interface similar to the 70's-era operating system.

UNIX: Origins

The UNIX operating system was born in late 1969 at AT&T's Bell Laboratories, in the shadow of Multics. Multics was a project intended to enable the commercialization of computing as a service, in a business model similar to telephone or electricity: Customers would connect their keyboard and monitor to a central mainframe, then pay for computation on an as-used basis.[1] Multics was a large, and in many ways, novel project. Developed at MIT, in collaboration with General Electric and AT&T, the project never realized its dream, and in 1969 AT&T withdrew its support.

At Bell Labs, in the aftermath of Multics, operating systems developers Kenneth Thompson, Dennis Ritchie, Douglas McIlroy, and Joe Ossanna found themselves without a clear project.[2] They proposed many ideas, but none were accepted, and no new machines were purchased.[2]

After finding a disused PDP-7, Ken Thompson and Dennis Ritchie rewrote the game Space Travel for it.[2] Thompson then continued to work with the machine, implementing a filesystem that he, Dennis Ritchie and Rudd Canaday had devised on paper.[2]

At some point in 1969, Thompson realized that he was close to an operating system: All it needed was an assembler, a text editor, a shell and an exec system call.[3] The exec call was "trivial", and the other three components were done in one week each.[3]

The software continued to be developed into a minimal, feasible, multi-user, time-sharing operating system, now known as UNIX. The novel system well outperformed for its size, and its popularity was due in part to its ability to run on varied and relatively inexpensive hardware.[4]

The UNIX Architecture

UNIX had a clear, simple, well-documented architecture that inspired many operating systems. It is organized as a hierarchy of layers, with each layer building upon the ones beneath it, and supporting the layers above it.

Hardware: The core of any computer is its hardware. Each piece of hardware has a software interface defined by the engineers that created it; these interfaces are varied at best.

Drivers: Drivers interact with hardware, and simplify the interface between kernel and hardware. These pieces of software are the deepest and most remote elements of any operating system.

Kernel: The heart of any UNIX-like operating system is its kernel. The kernel is responsible for scheduling the CPU, allotting memory, maintaining file integrity, and many other tasks. It is an involved piece of software, effectively, the "brains" of the operation.

System Calls: The set of operations that a kernel is willing to undertake is defined by the system calls it supports. The UNIX architecture is characterized by six essential system calls: fork, exec, open, close, read, and write.[5] Both fork and exec are concerned with launching programs, while open, close, read, and write are concerned with files.

Processes: A process is an executing program. Within the UNIX architecture, processes run the show: The kernel is entirely dependant upon processes to tell it what to do. Effectively, the kernel has no "mind" of its own.

GNU's Not Unix

Richard Stallman

The first attempt at producing a deliberately free and open-source UNIX clone was announced by Richard Stallman in 1983. Stallman had been working at MIT's AI Laboratory for over a decade, and had grown accustomed to its culture of sharing source code:

"If you saw someone using an unfamiliar and interesting program, you could always ask to see the source code, so that you could read it, change it, or cannibalize parts of it to make a new program."[6]

Yet, proprietary software was becoming the norm, with students working on projects intended for profit,[7] and companies going so far as to require nondisclosure agreements be signed to get an executable.[6] On September 27, 1983, Stallman announced his resolution: To produce a "complete UNIX-compatible software system," and to "give it away free."[8] He called the project GNU, a recursive acronym for GNU's Not UNIX.[9]

At the time of announcement, Stallman had not yet quantified his own intentions behind the word free. Certainly, "a free copy" did not embody the culture of code-sharing MIT was once home to. More, he did not wish to put the code in the public domain because modified versions of it it could become proprietary.[10] By 1985, Stallman had sorted these issues out:

GNU is not in the public domain. Everyone will be permitted to modify and redistribute GNU, but no distributor will be allowed to restrict its further redistribution. That is to say, proprietary modifications will not be allowed. I want to make sure that all versions of GNU remain free.[11]

Thus, the notion of free software was born: Users can run, study, modify, copy and distribute GNU, but if they distribute them, then those freedoms must be preserved. These stipulations were formalized in the GNU General Public License.

About it, Linus Torvalds would later write:

"Under the terms of the GPL, money is not the issue. You can charge a million bucks if someone is willing to pay for it, but you have to make sources available. And the person you give or sell the source to has to have all the rights you have. It's a brilliant device."[5]

Aside:

GNU's own kernel, Hurd, has not, as of 2021, been completed.

To create an entire operating system is an enormous task, but by 1990, the GNU Project had found or written all major components except one, the kernel.[9] Today, the GNU Project is renowned for producing high-quality, well-documented free software; they are as "official" as the open-source community gets. They remain active and prominent among the free software community, fighting the good fight with the help of the Free Software Foundation.[13] Richard Stallman remains Chief GNUisance.[13]

The Linux Kernel

On August 25, 1991, a Finnish graduate student by the name of Linus Torvalds announced his own free operating system project. Again following the architecture of the UNIX operating system, his project was intended to be "...just a hobby, won't be big and professional like GNU."[14]

Aside:

Properly speaking, the name Linux refers only to the Linux kernel.

Whereas the GNU Project sought to produce an entire operating system, Linus restricted his attention to the kernel. By October of 1991, GNU's Bash, GCC, Make and other programs were running on his creation.[14] He soon released it under the GNU General Public License, Version 2. Later, he would write:

"The fact is, to make Linux usable, I had relied on a lot of tools that had been distributed freely over the Internet—I had hoisted myself up on the shoulders of giants. The most important of these free software programs was the GCC compiler."[5]

Aside:

Linus Torvalds became interested in operating systems after finding an error in the source code of his Sinclair QL's QDOS. [5] In college, Andrew Tanenbaum's Operating Systems: Design and Implementation took this interest to new heights.[5]

Interest in the kernel flourished, in large part because of the informality of the project: Everybody was invited, if only to tinker. This development model— "release early and often, delegate everything you can, be open to the point of promiscuity"[15]— raised eyebrows at the time, but has proven sound in virtue of its overwhelming success: The model quickly came to dominate the open-source landscape, and the Linux kernel has become the most-used UNIX-like kernel of all time, powering supercomputers, webservers and nerds' laptops everywhere.

Linus Torvalds remains the maintainer of the Development branch, and often remarks upon the kernel's latest releases. Another public figure of today's Linux development effort is Greg Kroah-Hartman (pronounced "Crow-Hartman"). He is lead maintainer of the stable branch, in addition to various subsystems.[16] According to he, interest in the kernel continues to grow, with 1200 new developers contributing each year.[17] Their main development bottlenecks are code review and maintainership.[17]

Linux Distributions: A Brief History

The first versions of the Linux kernel were available only in source-code form, and required compilation on a separate host machine.[14] No other software was included, so that the creation of a functional Linux-based machine required the user to obtain, compile and assemble a number of disparate software components.

HJ Lu's Boot/Root Floppies

In 1992, HJ Lu began packaging utilities alongside the kernel and distributing the collection on a pair of 5.25" disks.[20][21] Known as the boot/root floppies, they are the first known Linux distribution.[21]

Linux's first widely recognized and used distribution was, ironically, a commercial venture, Soft Landing System.[22] Released in late 1992, SLS was sold under the slogan "Gentle Touchdowns for DOS Bailouts",[23] but users complained that the software was buggy,[23][24] and when its developers decided to change the executable file format, began to leave.[22]

Two Responses

In late 1992, Patrick Volkerding needed a LISP interpreter for a project at school, and, having heard that clisp ran on SLS Linux, downloaded them both.[24] His professor got wind, and asked Patrick to help install the software on school computers.[24] Volkerding walked him through installing, fixing and patching SLS, and afterward, his professor asked, "Is there some way we can fix the install disks so that the new machines will have these fixes right away?"[24]

So Volkerding began improving SLS, and internally named his project Slackware, not wanting it to be taken too seriously.[24] By May, though, the improvements were, "more than just cosmetic," and his friends convinced him to release it onto the internet. The first release proved so popular that the number of download requests crashed the system hosting it,[24] and Slackware would be, for several years, the de-facto Linux distribution.

Volkerding hadn't intended to produce a distribution,[24] but still maintains Slackware today.[25] The Slackware Linux Project is aimed at producing, "the most UNIX-like Linux distribution out there."[26]

Slackware 1.01, released 1993

At about the same time, Ian Murdock grew frustrated with SLS Linux,[27] and, more, sought a better solution to distribution creation than single-person or closed-room schemes.[27] Ian wanted an operating system that lived up to the Linux name, was carefully and conscientiously assembled, and which was maintained with equal diligence.[27] In particular, he wanted a free operating system that could compete with commercial distributions.[27]

Murdock decided to make the distribution creation process open, thereby dividing the burden of maintainership while ensuring satisfaction of users' requirements.[27] He named it Debian, a portmanteau of Debra, his wife, and Ian, himself.[28] The project attracted developers, and today around a thousand people volunteer their time.[29]

Debian is one of the most respected distributions of our time. The quantity of software available through its repositories is unparalleled, and it is oft-used as an upstream source for derivate distributions.

Debian 4 "Etch", released 2007

With the internet came many distributions. Since they are each based around the Linux kernel, and utilize key components given away by the GNU Project, we refer to them as GNU/Linux.

Linux Mint

Aside:

Mint is one of several Debian derivatives.

My favorite distribution is Linux Mint, but then, I'm a simple guy. Mint is a characteristically professional operating system, and focuses on productivity— that means you'll be up and running before you know it. It is extremely new-user friendly, yet maintains the performance of any other UNIX-like operating system.

Recently, Mint 19 began shipping with Timeshift, a tool that automaticaly takes snapshots of the filesystem, and allows the user to restore the system to some earlier date.

Mint offers the Cinnamon desktop environment; Windows users will feel right at home. I find it to be an attractive desktop, but it's only moderately customizable. Like many users, I want a desktop environment that stays out of my way, and Cinnamon does exactly that.

Linux Mint 20.1 "Ulyssa", Released 2021

What sets Mint apart from other distributions is that tools like Timeshift and Cinnamon are preinstalled and ready to use. When an example operating system is required in this book, Mint will be used, but similar remarks apply to virtually any distribution, with a little perusing.

Closing Remarks:

Open Source

In the forty years since Richard Stallman began GNU (pronounced guh-nu), the free software landscape has seen a great deal of growth. In 1998, the term open source was invented as a less abrasive synonym for free software:

We realized it was time to dump the confrontational attitude that has been associated with 'free software' in the past and sell the idea strictly on the same pragmatic, business-case grounds that motivated Netscape.[30]

The result was a wealth of media attention, the Open Source Initiative and the Mozilla project. Both GNU and the OSI agree that their respective terms are nearly synonymous.[31][32] In this work, and in life, we will use "open-source" interchangeably with "free software".

Aside:

Today, the most popular open-source license is MIT's, by far.[33]

In addition, more developers have taken part, and introduced a number of licenses to suit their needs. These licenses can be divided into two classes: Permissive licenses (e.g., MIT, Apache, BSD) do not perpetuate themselves, and so indirectly allow derivate works to become proprietary. Nonpermissive licenses (e.g., GPL, MPL), on the other hand, do perpetuate themselves, and so represent a type of copyleft.[34][35]

To be honest, a lot of developers have said, To hell with this, and introduced such novelties as the Beerware License, the Do What The **** You Want License, and the Unlicense (quite popular). These each place code in the public domain; from there, anyone can claim it as their own, and can put it in any other work, under any other license.
