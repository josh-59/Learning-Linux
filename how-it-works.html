How Open Source Works

"That's how open source works:
Iterate, deploy, study, repeat."
Ion Shell manual

So what makes it all tick? In this dramatic conclusion, we'll pick apart the key components of the open-source ecosystem, from the motivations of contributors to the leadership style necessarily implied by them. Disclaimer: The open-source community is so diverse that general statements about it don't usually hold true. What holds for many might not hold for a few. Consequently, this article principally conveys the author's opinion about its works.

An Analogy

Let's start by considering another hobby-centric collaboration that has produced free works: 3D Printing. The 3D printing community is composed principally (but not exclusively) of hobbyists, whom share plans in much the same way that the open-source ecosystem shares source.

Kirby Fume Extractor, by Thingiverse user jocoquemx
plans (and pictures) released under CC-By

While 3D printing is a term known by most people, most don't partake in it. We can hazard a guess that a normal person doesn't own a 3D printer, doesn't have a need for one, and doesn't care to make a hobby out of it. Similarly, most users of open-source do not partake in its development. The expertise required is nontrivial, and for the effort put into it, the gains are marginal (or at least, very specific).

We can carry this analogy one step further, and note that it is the plans that are shared freely within the maker community, and not any physical item. Source code is a similar kind of "plan," and is realized through execution.

In comparison to the maker community, open source is older, has fielded much larger cooperative efforts, and has produced much more sophisticated entities. The largest open-source project is neither GNU nor Linux, but Apache, a collection of public domain software that underpins the internet.

Collaborative Open-Source

Aside:

Recall that open source is a synonym for free software.

Properly speaking, open-source describes only how software is distributed and used. Notably, it requires human-readable "source" be made available alongside machine-readable instructions, and that software be redistributable by any user.

In particular, it does not restrict the organizational structure adopted to produce these works, and many open-source projects are not open to collaboration. For example, GNU is not collaborative: While users are permitted to modify and redistribute the project's works, those modifications were never intended to make their way back into GNU source. Hence, there is a deliberate delineation between user and developer.

Collaborative Open Source

When Linus Torvalds invited any interested hacker to tinker on his kernel, he invented a new way of making free software. The result was "Accidentally Revolutionary," and has dominated open-source since.

Within his project, the heavy lifting is carried out by collaborators— People who, having a desire, implement it themselves, then make the change available back to its source. It should be noted that there is no "rule" that collaboration is restricted to hobbyists, and being inclusive with respect to organizations fosters project success. Facebook, for example, has put considerable effort into Linux's new btrfs.[h]

Collaborative open-source blurs the line between "user" and "developer": You can get as involved as you want. The advantages of this organizational structure are that developers are distributed, organizational costs are minimized, and individuals or groups may contribute to a project according to their own desires.

In practice, most modifications (~80%) are made by a small group of people. More, most collaborators are "casual," offering one or two commits to a project over its life. Finally, the vast majority of users are not collaborators at all; it is not the case that open-source is powered by a collection of average users.

Code and money are not the only ways to support your favorite open-source project. Documentation, translation, and project promotion are all universally appreciated. In addition, the best programming aide that a developer can receive is detailed bug reports.

Intrinsic Motivation

Compared to the market economy we all know and love, open source a strange beast: Its works are carried out because what they are is what their creators want. A reasonable question to ask, is, Why? as in, Why take seriously and labor for something without obvious monetary reward?

Economist Steven Weber offers some insight:

"Human beings often have a passionate relationship to their creative endeavors and their work; they wish to share their creativity with others; and value inheres in things other than monetizable rewards. Each of these attitudes exists within the open source community."[g]

Collaborative open-source is not necessarily altruistic. While the motivations of an individual are, naturally, their own, and may well be strictly charitable, those projects that are most enduring satisfy some community need, and are supported in virtue of themselves.

As an example, the util-linux package was originally forked from the BSD project, and passed through several maintainers between versions 2.2 and 2.12.[g] When it was clear that the project had been abandoned, it was picked up again by Karel Zak, whom continues to maintain it today. In this way, those projects which are of interest to the community are maintained, while those that are not are eventually dropped.

Curating Work

The open-source ecosystem has naturally decomposed into projects, with people working on what they want. While we may wish to think that each project is a paradigm of virtue and cooperation, this is in fact not the case. Public desire is often not homogenous, and the role of project leader can quickly skew from lead developer to modification curator.

Aside:

The term maintainer is often used to refer to the position of project leader.

Although it may seem minor, this is the key point, the primary difference between Linus' model and every other way of developing software: Changes are submitted by individuals to projects, inspected on a case-by-case basis, and then either accepted into the project or rejected. Project leaders take on the responsibility of their work's overall well-being, and can modify the code however they see fit; they foster its development by considering proposed modifications.

While users are in theory empowered to shape a project into what they want, in practice these modifications are moderated by the project's maintainers. Given significant difference of opinion, an individual or group can always fork the source repository and lead a sister project in their preferred direction. For example, the Experimental/Enhanced GNU Compiler System was a fork of GNU's compiler collection that included new languages in a manner more free-wheeling than the FSF found acceptable. Through this mechanism, users (collectively speaking) control the programs available.

In a nontrivial sense, this ability to choose and curate is the backbone of open-source, and takes place on multiple levels. A distribution implicitly curates projects in their presentation of an operating system, while users curate distributions, projects and source, however they see fit.

Working Together

Each proposed edit filters up towards a more senior developer on its way to being accepted. Linus himself has attained the vaunted role of Benevolent Dictator for Life.

A Success Story

A paging utility is a program that displays a file on the terminal one screenful at a time. The first widely used paging utility was more, written by Dan Halbert as a graduate student at UC Berkley in 1978.[aa] more is a relatively simple program: It first fills up the screen, then waits for user input to print the next line(s).

In 1983, Mark Nudelman was a software developer working for a company called Integrated Office Systems. From the less FAQ:

Some of our software ran on a Unix system and produced enormous log files of its transactions. We wanted to be able to search these log files for error messages. But the version of "vi" we were using couldn't handle files larger than a certain size, and our log files often exceeded that size. So we were forced to use "more" to view the files. The problem was, once we found an error message, what we really wanted to see was the transactions leading up to the error; that is, the lines in the log file immediately before the error message. But more didn't allow backward movement in the file.
We often complained about this problem. We said we needed a "backwards more"; someone (it wasn't me, but unfortunately I don't remember who it was) coined the name "less" as a joke on the concept of a "backwards more". It didn't seem to me that it would be too difficult to write a simple pager that would allow forward and backward movement. I wrote the first version in late 1983 and we began using it internally. I continued to enhance it and finally decided it was a useful enough tool that I should make it available to the world. I posted the first version to the newsgroup called (at that time) net.sources in May, 1985.
Making the program publicly available allowed an enormous number of people to use it. Many people have made their own modifications and donated them to me for incorporation into the official version, and many more have reported bugs they've found or made suggestions about features they'd like to see added. This was my first experience with the concept of what is now called "open source" or "free software", and it has, I believe, produced a very high quality product. I'd like to thank all the users of less for their invaluable comments and suggestions over the years. Less wouldn't be what it is today without you.

Mark Nudelman continues to maintain the project today. It is, at the time of this writing, in its 581st version.[q][r]
