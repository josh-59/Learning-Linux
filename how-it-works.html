How It Works

"Given enough eyeballs, all bugs are shallow."
Linus Torvalds

So what makes it all tick? In this dramatic conclusion, we'll pick apart the key components of the open-source ecosystem, from the motivations of contributors to the leadership style necessarily implied by them.

Low Copy Cost

In order to portray the success of open-source with any degree of honesty, we must first admit that it is dependant upon the low (virtually zero) cost of reproducing a piece of software.

Intrinsic Motivation

Compared to the market economy we all know and love, open source a strange beast: Its works are created because what they are is what their creators want. Stated another way, the intent of all free software is the software itself.

Note:

Here we are adopting a (very) high-level perspective of the open-source ecosystem. By analogy, money, rather than passion, enjoyment, idealism, etc., motivates work in the market economy.

Admittedly, contributing to open source sometimes makes sound financial sense— Facebook taking part in the development of btrfs is one such example. However, in this case, as in all other cases, the principal interest of Facebook is in the filesystem itself: It is developed to be used, not to be sold.

Goods produced for their own sake are said to be intrinsically motivated, and this, in turn, underpins the open-source ecosystem. Intrinsic motivation is found in many places, from research in academia to artistic works on public display.

The open-source ecosystem has naturally decomposed into projects, with people working on what they want. While we may like to think that open-source is strictly the work of hobbyists, in practice this isn't the case. Since the resulting software may be used for any purpose— including commercial purposes— it has naturally come to pass that some developers intend to profit from its use.

From the above, it would follow that those projects with the greatest number of use-cases garner the greatest support, and this I hold to be true: To date, the largest free-software organization in existence is the Apache Foundation, whose projects underpin the world wide web.

Distributed Collaboration

The term open-source describes how software is distributed and used— Not how it is produced.[a] While it requires that source code be available to users for perusal, modification and redistribution, it does not require that the project itself be collaborative, and many open-source projects are not open to collaboration.

While collaboration is neither part of the open-source definition nor part of the original intent of free software, it is both the novel component of today's open-source ecosystem and, in the author's opinion, has been pivotal to its success. Therefore, we will pursue it here.

Aside:

The "tools and materials" observation is owed to Dr. Weber, as well.

First, collaboration has been made possible: Anybody with an internet connection can download and use free software, and users are permitted to modify it to suit their needs. Since the collection of free software includes all development tools required to make modifications, both tools and materials are made available.

Second, collaboration is supported by project leaders. To make clear, a project's leader is the individual whom releases the official product. Notably, they alone can modify the code however they see fit.

Since each leader has the right to modify their work however they see fit, they have the right to decline a modification for any reason whatsoever. In practice, predominantly collaborative projects are led by curating proposed modifications: The direction of the project is clearly stated, and the terms of acceptable modifications are made plain. It should be noted, however, that even in collaborative open-source, the majority of contributions come from a relatively small pool of core developers; concurrently, most users do not submit modifications.

Aside:

Since collaboration is fun, you could take the view that it, also, is intrinsically motivated.

Finally, collaboration is just fun: There's a strong social component to it, a creative component to it, challenge and reward. Through "social coding" websites like Github and Gitlab, it has even been made convenient. Although programming itself is difficult, the act of submitting a modification can be done by anyone, anywhere.

Modularity

The final piece of the open-source ecosystem puzzle is modularity— specifically, the decomposition of a large and complex whole into manageable pieces. One of the truly remarkable things of modern open-source is that no one person or organization is responsible for the creation of a GNU/Linux operating system. Rather, collecting and assembling the pieces is a project of its own, distinct from, say, shell development, itself distinct from, say, kernel development.

This modularization occurred naturally as the free-software ecosystem became self-supporting, and it is, in the author's opinion, fundamental to the pattern of open-source success. - Working on what they want - Even large organizations tend to compartmentalize by project...

The success of any project, with respect to producing a mature and polished product, is contingent principally upon its improvement through long-term evolution. The modularity of GNU/Linux has allowed it to mature by allowing individual projects to come and go.

A Success Story

A paging utility is a program that displays a file on the terminal one screenful at a time. The first widely used paging utility was more, written by Dan Halbert as a graduate student at UC Berkeley in 1978.[aa] more is a simple program: It first fills up the screen, then waits for user input to print the next line(s).

In 1983, Mark Nudelman was a software developer working for a company called Integrated Office Systems. From the less FAQ:

Some of our software ran on a Unix system and produced enormous log files of its transactions. We wanted to be able to search these log files for error messages. But the version of "vi" we were using couldn't handle files larger than a certain size, and our log files often exceeded that size. So we were forced to use "more" to view the files. The problem was, once we found an error message, what we really wanted to see was the transactions leading up to the error; that is, the lines in the log file immediately before the error message. But more didn't allow backward movement in the file.
We often complained about this problem. We said we needed a "backwards more"; someone (it wasn't me, but unfortunately I don't remember who it was) coined the name "less" as a joke on the concept of a "backwards more". It didn't seem to me that it would be too difficult to write a simple pager that would allow forward and backward movement. I wrote the first version in late 1983 and we began using it internally. I continued to enhance it and finally decided it was a useful enough tool that I should make it available to the world. I posted the first version to the newsgroup called (at that time) net.sources in May, 1985.
Making the program publicly available allowed an enormous number of people to use it. Many people have made their own modifications and donated them to me for incorporation into the official version, and many more have reported bugs they've found or made suggestions about features they'd like to see added. This was my first experience with the concept of what is now called "open source" or "free software", and it has, I believe, produced a very high quality product. I'd like to thank all the users of less for their invaluable comments and suggestions over the years. Less wouldn't be what it is today without you.

Mark Nudelman continues to maintain the project today. It is, at the time of this writing, in its 581st version.[q][r]

How it Fails

By and large, the health of a project can be determined by how vigorously it is being developed: As Ken Thompson said, "Code by itself almost rots and it's gotta be rewritten. Even when nothing has changed, for some reason it rots."[e] Simply put, if it isn't under active development, then it's been abandoned.
