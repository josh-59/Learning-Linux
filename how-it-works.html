How Open Source Works

"That's how open source works:
Iterate, deploy, study, repeat."
Ion Shell manual

So what makes it all tick? In this dramatic conclusion, we'll pick apart the key components of the open-source ecosystem, from the motivations of contributors to the leadership style necessarily implied by them.

An Analogy

Let's start by ignoring the actual software part of open source, and consider another ecosystem that produces free works: 3D Printing. The 3D printing community is composed principally (but not exclusively) of hobbiests, whom share plans in much the same way that the open-source ecosystem shares source.

The typical plan is not sophisticated, and is principally a creative endeavor. About this, economist ______ Weber says,

"Human beings often have a passionate relationship to their creative endeavors and their work; they wish to share their creativity with others; and value inheres in things other than monetizable rewards. Each of these attitudes exists within the open source community."[g]

In contrast to the maker community, open source is older, has produced much more sophisticated entities through much larger cooperative efforts, and is comparatively well-studied. We have found that most people do not contribute, and even those that do contribute tend to contribute just once or twice.

Organizational Structures

Aside:

Recall that open source is a synonym for free software.

Properly speaking, open-source describes only how software is distributed and used— Not how it is produced. Notably, it requires human-readable "source" be made available alongside machine-readable instructions, and that software be redistributable by any user.

In particular, the organizational structure adopted to produce this software is not restricted, and many open-source projects are not open to collaboration. For example, GNU is not collaborative: While users are permitted to modify and redistribute the project's works, those modifications were never intended to make their way back into GNU source, and they usually do not.

Hence, there is a deliberate delineation between user and developer. The GNU project sets itself apart from other non-profit organizations in that its products are made impactful in virtue of their low "marginal" cost: It costs very little to create another copy, and thereby support another user.

Collaborative Open Source

When Linus Torvalds invited any interested hacker to tinker on his kernel, he invented a new way of making free software. The result was "Accidentally Revolutionary," and has dominated open-source since.

Within his project, the heavy lifting is carried out by user-contributors— People who, having a desire, implement it themselves, then make the change available back to its source. It should be noted that there is no "rule" that collaboration be restricted to hobbyists. Facebook, for example, has put considerable effort into Linux's new btrfs.

Collaborative open-source blurs the line between "user" and "developer": You can get as involved as you want. In practice, most contributors are casual, offering just one or two changes, with a relatively small cadre tasking themselves with ensuring project coherence.

Organizing Work:

Bazaar Style

The open-source ecosystem has naturally decomposed into projects, with people working on what they want. Without the clear external motivation of money, collaborative open source is said to be intrinsically motivated: They are carried out because what they are is what their creators want.

While we may wish to think that each project is a paradigm of virtue and cooperation, this is in fact not the case. Public desire is not homogenous, and, especially in large projects, the role of project leader can quickly skew from lead developer to modification curator.

This is the key point, and the primary difference between Linus' model and every other way of working: In collaborative open-source, work is submitted by users, inspected on a case-by-case basis, and then either accepted into the project or rejected. Project leaders take on the responsibility of the work's overall well-being, and foster its development by sifting through (and modifying) proposed modifications.

Note:

The term maintainer is usually used to refer to project leader.

So, while individuals are, in theory, empowerd to shape a project into what they want, in practice these modifications are moderated by the project's maintainers. Given significant difference of opinion, users can always fork the source repository and lead a sister project in their preferred direction.

Even supposing perfect cooperation, many disconnected but well-meaning contributors work in parallel, not in concert— they're each modifying the same sheet, essentially. Consequently, small changes are much preferred, with the result being highly evolutionary, and rarely revolutionary, improvement.

Within Linus' project, those dedicated maintainers form of a "tree of trust" to handle code review and commit acceptance: Each edit suggested by someone outside the group filters up towards the more seniour developers, on their way to being accepted.

Time-based Releases

Large collaborative projects, for whom many people want to include changes, often schedule releases. This pattern accomodates both feature introduction and commit rejection: If your new feature doesn't make it into the latest version of Firefox, for instance, you only have to wait six weeks for the next chance.

Mozilla calls this, "stability without stagnation."[6]

Another example: Debian Sid

For example, the last opportunity to merge feature changes in Firefox is before it enters beta. In beta, merges are restricted to bug fixes and security updates. Each release spends four weeks in beta before being released as the current official Firefox version.

While it is being used by the public, merges are again restricted to bug fixes and security updates, and, again, each release is supported for four weeks:

Witin Mozilla, this is known as a "train schedule," and we see that Firefox 80 will be released as Firefox 81 enters Beta and Firefox 82 enters Nightly.

Firefox is one of the largest and most agressive of open-source projects— It even spawned the (awesome) programming language, Rust.

less:

A Success Story

A paging utility is a program that displays a file on the terminal one screenful at a time. The first widely used paging utility was more, written by Dan Halbert as a graduate student at UC Berkley in 1978.[aa] more is a relatively simple program: It first fills up the screen, then waits for user input to print the next line(s).

In 1983, Mark Nudelman was a software developer working for a company called Integrated Office Systems. From the less FAQ:

Some of our software ran on a Unix system and produced enormous log files of its transactions. We wanted to be able to search these log files for error messages. But the version of "vi" we were using couldn't handle files larger than a certain size, and our log files often exceeded that size. So we were forced to use "more" to view the files. The problem was, once we found an error message, what we really wanted to see was the transactions leading up to the error; that is, the lines in the log file immediately before the error message. But more didn't allow backward movement in the file.
We often complained about this problem. We said we needed a "backwards more"; someone (it wasn't me, but unfortunately I don't remember who it was) coined the name "less" as a joke on the concept of a "backwards more". It didn't seem to me that it would be too difficult to write a simple pager that would allow forward and backward movement. I wrote the first version in late 1983 and we began using it internally. I continued to enhance it and finally decided it was a useful enough tool that I should make it available to the world. I posted the first version to the newsgroup called (at that time) net.sources in May, 1985.
Making the program publicly available allowed an enormous number of people to use it. Many people have made their own modifications and donated them to me for incorporation into the official version, and many more have reported bugs they've found or made suggestions about features they'd like to see added. This was my first experience with the concept of what is now called "open source" or "free software", and it has, I believe, produced a very high quality product. I'd like to thank all the users of less for their invaluable comments and suggestions over the years. Less wouldn't be what it is today without you.

Mark Nudelman continues to maintain the project today. It is, at the time of this writing, in its 581st version.[q][r]

Working in Open Source

Open-source is a strange beast: Its works were built at the behest of their creators. Counterintuitively, those that are most successful and enduring are carried out for their own sake.

Apparently, she thought that Redox OS would be the next big thing. ...Apparently, she thought that Debian would make the world a better place.

Do what you Want.

Especially in the case of large projects, one can expect to find that they are not alone in their desire for it. The consequence is both appreciation for the work, and if it is large enough to require collaboration, collaboration.

Make it work first, then make it work better, From filesystems to operating systems... Even Linus Torvalds started off writing A and B on his 386.

Do one thing and do it well. Precisely state your project's goal.

The most important component is reiterative improvement.

A Sport

To call open-source a hobby isn't quite strong enough. At the same time, it isn't work in the traditional sense, either. I think it's somewhere between the two, closer to sport.

Most people can make a program work— and programming is a great hobby and a useful skill— but the software that actually gets used on a daily basis (e.g., Bash) is extremely sophisticated. These projects are shepherded by talented and educated individuals, whom are professionals of their field. The magic is that they make the things they do because they want to.

Code and money are not the only ways to support your favorite open-source project, and I would encourage you to help in any way you can. Detailed bug reports, if you find them. More, I would encourage you to find something you want to do, and make it happen.
