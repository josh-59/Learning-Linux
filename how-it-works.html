How It Works

"Given enough eyeballs, all bugs are shallow."
Linus Torvalds

So what makes it all tick? In this dramatic conclusion, we'll pick apart the key components of the open-source ecosystem, from the motivations of contributors to the leadership style necessarily implied by them. Note: This article focuses on collaborative open-source, and ignores non-collaborative (e.g., "Cathedral") organization.

Non-Rival

Our first task is to describe the product being distributed.

Software is non-rival: This means that it can be used by many people as easily as it can be used by one. A good example of a non-rival good is a television broadcast: It may be watched by one or it may be watched by many without the broadcaster making any distinction.

"The purpose of [a] model is not to represent reality; its purpose is to place in sharp relief particular elements of reality so we can look at the model from different angles, tweak it in various directions, generalize it, and then come back to reality with a deeper understanding of what happens there."[a]

Right to Distribute

Intrinsic Motivation

Remember:

We want what it is.

Compared to the market economy we all know and love, open source a strange beast: Its works are created because what they are is what their creators want. Stated another way, the intent of all free software is the software itself. Goods produced for their own sake are said to be intrinsically motivated, and this underpins the open-source ecosystem. Intrinsic motivation is found in many other places, such as academic research and artistic works set on public display.

Admittedly, we are adopting a very high-level perspective of the open-source ecosystem: Many contributors to the free software ecosystem have other motivations, such as passion, enjoyment, idealism, etc.. Nonetheless, it is the author's opinion that intrinsic motivation serves the same fundamental purpose to the open-source ecosystem that money serves within the market economy.

Contributing to open source sometimes makes sound financial sense— Facebook taking part in the development of btrfs is one such example. However, in this case, as in most other cases, the principal interest of Facebook is in the filesystem itself: It is developed to be used, not to be sold, and is therefore developed for its intrinsic value, rather than direct profit.

If intrinsic motivation indeed serves as the principle motivation of open-source software, then it would follow that those projects with the greatest number of use-cases garner the greatest support. To date, the largest free-software organization in existence is neither GNU nor Linux, but the Apache Foundation, whose projects underpin the world wide web.

Voluntary

Aside:

No distinction is made between user and developer. However, most users are not developers.

The members of the open-source community work together in a highly parallel and nearly unstructured fashion. Weber lists this as the defining characteristic of the open-source process:

The key element of the open source process, as an ideal type, is the voluntary participation and voluntary selection of tasks.[sic] Anyone can join an open source project, and anyone can leave at any time. That is not just a free market in labor. What makes it different from the theoretical option of exit from a corporate organization is this: Each person is free to choose what he wishes to work on or to contribute. There is no consciously organized or enforced division of labor.

Each person is presented with the same core code-base— the project as it exists presently— and can modify it however they desire. A project maintainer may suggest things that could use improvement, but no user is obliged to make those changes, and any user can make other modifications.

Modularity

Since Richard Stallman began the GNU project, the open-source ecosystem has grown, with people working on the things they want. While we may like to think that open-source is strictly the work of hobbyists, in practice this isn't the case. Since the resulting software may be used for any purpose— including commercial purposes— it has naturally come to pass that some developers intend to profit from its use.

The result is totally decentralized: There are relatively few authorities over the open-source ecosystem.

One of the truly remarkable things of modern open-source is that no one person or organization is responsible for the creation of a GNU/Linux operating system. Rather, collecting and assembling the pieces is a project of its own, distinct from, say, shell development, itself distinct from, say, kernel development.

Anatomy of a Project

Can even depend upon platform. GNU: Github/JS: Microprojects w/ one contributor

Aside:

An open-source software project without a maintainer is said to be unmaintained. It has been effectively abandoned (and/or is looking for a maintainer :).

Typically, though, a piece of free software will have one trusted maintainer. This person is responsible for presenting the project to the world, and for releasing new versions as they become mature. Maintainers have the right to modify their work however they see fit, can accept users' modifications however they see fit, and have the right to decline a modification for any reason whatsoever. In practice, discussion usually precedes modification. After modifications are submitted, they are reviewed on a case-by-case basis, and then either accepted or rejected.

The success of any project, with respect to producing a mature and polished product, is contingent principally upon its improvement through long-term evolution.

A Success Story

A paging utility is a program that displays a file on the terminal one screenful at a time. The first widely used paging utility was more, written by Dan Halbert as a graduate student at UC Berkeley in 1978.[aa] more is a simple program: It first fills up the screen, then waits for user input to print the next line(s).

In 1983, Mark Nudelman was a software developer working for a company called Integrated Office Systems. From the less FAQ:

Some of our software ran on a Unix system and produced enormous log files of its transactions. We wanted to be able to search these log files for error messages. But the version of "vi" we were using couldn't handle files larger than a certain size, and our log files often exceeded that size. So we were forced to use "more" to view the files. The problem was, once we found an error message, what we really wanted to see was the transactions leading up to the error; that is, the lines in the log file immediately before the error message. But more didn't allow backward movement in the file.
We often complained about this problem. We said we needed a "backwards more"; someone (it wasn't me, but unfortunately I don't remember who it was) coined the name "less" as a joke on the concept of a "backwards more". It didn't seem to me that it would be too difficult to write a simple pager that would allow forward and backward movement. I wrote the first version in late 1983 and we began using it internally. I continued to enhance it and finally decided it was a useful enough tool that I should make it available to the world. I posted the first version to the newsgroup called (at that time) net.sources in May, 1985.
Making the program publicly available allowed an enormous number of people to use it. Many people have made their own modifications and donated them to me for incorporation into the official version, and many more have reported bugs they've found or made suggestions about features they'd like to see added. This was my first experience with the concept of what is now called "open source" or "free software", and it has, I believe, produced a very high quality product. I'd like to thank all the users of less for their invaluable comments and suggestions over the years. Less wouldn't be what it is today without you.

Mark Nudelman continues to maintain the project today. It is, at the time of this writing, in its 581st version.[q][r]
