Learning Linux How-to

Under Construction!

Learning GNU/Linux is not necessarily easy. Although there exists many sources of documentation, they are not, in general, well-organized, so that searching for the right source can be hard. This paper aims to give an overview of available resources, some useful pointers for each, and links, as appropriate.

How-to

Steps:

  1. Figure out what your question is.

    This can be more difficult than you might guess, because you might honestly not know how to phrase your question. Be specific! Things are very well-defined in computers, and this stands in great contrast to the novice's intuitive understanding of their operation.

  2. How you can get an answer to it?
    • What is an appropriate source?
      • Man page
      • Official Documentation
      • Reference Manual
    • The application's manual?
    • Do you need more information than that?
    • Would an example be helpful?
    • Do you need more specific information or more general information?
  3. Find good sources to answer that question.

    Do not settle for the first mediocre, ad-ridden techno-blog that Google serves up! If you want to get better at this stuff,

Man Pages

The most useful assets in the Linux documentation chain are the manual ("man") pages: They are concise and immediately available— they are stored locally on your machine, and they usually comes with each installed application. They are accessed at the command line so:

man app

Once within the man utility, you can search the man pages by typing a forward slash, then your search term:

/<search-term>

Many of the controls of the man-pages coincide with those of the less paging utility, and with those of the vi/vim text editor, so they're worth learning. For instance, move down the page one line by typing j. Move up the page one line by typing k. And, move down a screenful by hitting the spacebar.

Man pages actually date back to the first implementations of Unix, where they served as the principle user manual for the system.
