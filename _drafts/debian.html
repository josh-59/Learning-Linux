In 1993, Ian Murdock grew frustrated with SLS Linux— Not only was it a commercial venture, it was also, "...quite possibly the most bug-ridden and badly maintained Linux distribution available."[29] That it was, additionally, the most popular distribution was, "...a bad combination indeed."[29]

Ian envisioned a modular operating system, whose components could be maintained by people with appropriate specialities and desires. By making the project open, the burden of maintainership could be distributed. More, both the persistence of the project and the satisfaction of users' requirements are assured.[29]

So Ian announced Debian:

"Debian Linux is a brand-new kind of Linux distribution. Rather than being developed by one isolated individual or group, as other distributions of Linux have been developed in the past, Debian is being developed openly in the spirit of Linux and GNU. The primary purpose of the Debian project is to finally create a distribution that lives up to the Linux name. Debian is being carefully and conscientiously put together and will be maintained and supported with similar care.

Debian has become one of the most respected distributions of our time. It is oft-used as an upstream source for derivate distributions, and the quantity of software available through its repositories is unparalleled.

UNIX: A Brief History: Much better source: Ken Thompson, Dennis Ritchie and Rudd Canaday drafted the filesystem.[a]

About his colleague's early work, Dennis Ritchie would later remark,

Thompson wanted to create a comfortable computing environment constructed according to his own design, using whatever means were available. His plans, it is evident in retrospect, incorporated many of the innovative aspects of Multics, including an explicit notion of a process as a locus of control, a tree-structured file system, a command interpreter as user-level program, simple representation of text files, and generalized access to devices.[4]

In 1993, Ian Murdock also grew frustrated with SLS Linux— its bugs, closed development model and commercial nature.[29] Ian wanted an operating system that lived up to the Linux name, which was carefully and conscientiously assembled, and which was maintained with equal diligence.[29] In particular, he wanted a free operating system that could compete with commercial distributions.[29]

Murdock envisioned a modular operating system, developed openly, "... in the spirit of GNU and Linux," which ensured the satisfaction of user requirements, and spread the burden of maintainership.[29] He named it Debian, a portmanteau of Debra, his wife, and Ian, himself.[30]

The project attracted developers, and today around a thousand people volunteer their spare time.[31] Debian is one of the most respected distributions of our time: The quantity of software available through its repositories is unparalleled, and it is oft-used as an upstream source for derivate distributions.
