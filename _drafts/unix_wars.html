Propogation

In 1973, Ken Thompson and Dennis Ritchie published the first paper about their work, The Unix Time-Sharing System. Owing to anti-monopoly legislation, AT&T was forbidden from profitting from the software, and Unix was not made available as a product per se; from 1973 to 1984, the latest version of Unix, including source, was available for a $150 administrative fee.

The work was of interest, especially, to academics, who, for the first time, could afford to purchase a computer— by department. The license it was distributed under permitted free use within an organization, "provided the users do not disclose outside the organization."[a] It was taught by professors and studied by college students as both a functional tool and an exemplar operating system.

Fun Fact:

Lion's Commentary on Unix 6th Edition was published two years before K&R.

In 1975 Ken Thompson took a sabatical from Bell Laboraties to teach at the University of California Berkley. His first task, of course, was installing Unix on their PDP-Something Something. Ken Thompson taught for two years, sufficient time to get a fork of UNIX off the ground. Known as the Berkley Software Distribution, it began at Unix source and diverged upon the efforts of professors and students alike.

Improvements were welcome from any source, and the BSD project marked the first time that software creation was open and distributed. Over traditional Unix, BSD boasted networking capabilities, a faster filesystem, better virtual memory handling, and support for a larger variety of programming languages.[b]
